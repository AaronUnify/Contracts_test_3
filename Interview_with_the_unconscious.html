Where does social media get its ideas from? It gets them from the traumatic experiences of people.

If instead of processing traumatic memories, the person lets them lay unexplored in the mind, the memories will constantly resurface and destroy the person's ability to focus any time something related to the negative experience occurs. This is because the mind is unaware of when the memory is actually useful and sends it to the surface as a warning any time something categorically related to the trauma happens. Because the memory is unprocessed it lives in the episodic memory system and the experience of remembering the memory is similar to actually reliving the experience.

The constant reliving of negative experience everytime something even scarecly related occurs can create a strong distrust of anything related to the unknown. Causing anything outside of the person't direct positive experience to be treated with fear and hostility.

When a person is looking at social media the algorithm tries to keep their attention for as long as possible. The algorithm sends content related to the posts that previously managed to keep our attention the longest. A picture or video on a phone screen that is only just related to the person's trauma may be tolerable. Putting the user on the edge of interested and terrified.

Videos that provide confirmation bias to ideas that we are yet to fully formulate tend to keep us watching longer. If the idea is something like, people who are members of certain group are all bad, the algorithm sends videos related to that idea.

Instead of processing your specific memories, you can watch videos that show similar events happening to other people. This gives the person many examples backed by the emotional potency of unprocessed trauma. Eventually the algorithm will send a video where someone upacks the ideas in careful language indicating that there is a general truth to the persons misguided intuition, giving a robust rational to go with your many examples.

Why not throw in an online community related to hating the outgroup that exists in an echo chamber where the members try to out-compete each by rewording the same set of ideas thousands of times over and over for good measure.

Where does social media get it's ideas from? If we're lucky, figuring out its nature may provide insight into ourselves. Maybe social media is not the collective beliefs of the people. Maybe there is independence to its actions that has emerged.

I started by assuming the left was public belief and the right was private belief. But politics can't possibly be all there is to what's happening here. Social media itself appears to have a structure that encourages politics and misguided thinking. I kind of wish I didn't start unpacking these ideas because the original premise was so easy, but I'll go as far as I can from here. I think we're nearly at the end.

The mind and social media are related in that they both exist in the information realm. The mind uses processed memories and unprocessed memories in the form of generalities when simulating the world. The mind is simulating the world for the purpose of prediction.

There is a clue to whats going on with social media in the name. Social, means that there is an attempt to take on the qualities of society. Media, indicates externally generated fragments of ideas. The name social media indicates many people sharing fragments of ideas. It doesn't take a genius to realise that this is a space that is open to political manipulation. Especially considering the threshold for evidence is so low, that a screenshot is considered evidence of the guilt of an entire group of people.

Social media uses an attention-seeking algorithm to lure people down slithers of a secondary reality. These secondary realities are not predictive, just projections of one part of a user's self. Whatever it is that your timeline shows, is an artificially generated reflection of your current interests and history, even if you don't know it. However, the timeline you see is dependent on the algorithm's ability to read you. When the algorithm makes a mistake, it may show content that is irrelevant to your history and interests.

Of course, compassionate and caring people dominate the public discussion on these platforms. Their opinions are not only safe to share publicly they can improve your social standing.
But any space for competition brings competitive people to the party. They begin to post increasingly compassionate content in an attempt to outdo one another. This leads to an unconscious resentment building in the crowd. They sense the scales have been tipped from the truth and social games are being played. After all, we just can't care about everything, the world is too big. Sometimes you should just let a crisis stay as private and as local as possible so the people involved can sort it out.

A million people trying to outdo each other in displays of increasingly absurd compassion usually only harms the people actually involved. In comes a helpful person to tip public discourse back towards truth, but oh no! They used too much pressure. Now everyone's upset and they need to make an apology. Meanwhile, the compassion competition campaign continues uninterrupted because no one is asked to apologize for compassion.

This process will go on until all the causes with the good victim groups are taken. Hopefully, after that, it'll taper off, because a competitive push to the frontiers of compassion to find groups to be compassionate towards is an absurd game in the long-term. Eventually, the social media world, followed by the real world, will turn into a landscape of identity either sanctioned or unsanctioned by social media.

Each time the mass of people agree a small underbelly of disagreers emerges. Instead of posting publicly the heretics message in private chats, slowly building trust and developing their ideas. Trust is required to form mutual expression of opinion when that opinion could be controversial. Once an underbelly of disagreers is at the point of generating memes the building blocks of their ideas are shared amongst increasing numbers of people using humor to make the publicly unpalatable idea palatable.

Slowly in the social media timeline, these counter-ideas start to appear tentatively. Eventually, someone takes it too far, and boom! Massive apology in order, this can be a devastating blow to some careers. But strangely not others.
